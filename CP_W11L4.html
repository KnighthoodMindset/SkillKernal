Privacy: The Indian Way - Part 1

Data Privacy in India

Data privacy in India is a multi-layered and evolving issue involving individuals, organizations, and the government. With the rapid digital transformation over the past two to three decades, privacy concerns have become increasingly prominent. India’s approach to privacy is grounded in its Constitution, particularly Article 21, which guarantees the right to life and personal liberty** but allows restrictions based on the “procedure established by law.” This constitutional framework has historically created a tension between individual rights and state authority, especially in matters concerning surveillance and national security. Early privacy-related legislation, such as the Indian Telegraph Act of 1885, granted the government the power to intercept communications in the interest of public safety. These powers were expanded in 1972, particularly during the Emergency period (1975–1977), highlighting how state security has often taken precedence over personal privacy.

Recognition of Privacy as a Fundamental Right

A major turning point came in 2017 with the Justice K.S. Puttaswamy vs. Union of India judgment, where the Supreme Court of India formally recognized privacy as a fundamental right under Article 21. This landmark ruling emerged during debates surrounding the Aadhaar system, a government initiative designed to collect and use biometric and demographic data of over 1.3 billion Indian citizens. While Aadhaar improved efficiency in welfare delivery and fraud prevention, it also sparked intense concerns about surveillance, data misuse, and violation of privacy. Citizens and experts questioned whether the government’s mandatory collection of biometric data for accessing essential services infringed upon individual privacy. This controversy led to the recognition that privacy and state interests must be balanced, ensuring that technological and administrative progress does not come at the cost of fundamental rights.

Legislative Developments and Policy Evolution

India’s privacy framework has gradually evolved through policy initiatives and legislative efforts. In 2016, the Justice B.N. Srikrishna Committee was established to draft a comprehensive data protection law, drawing inspiration from the European Union’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). The committee’s work led to the Personal Data Protection (PDP) Bill, which sought to regulate how organizations collect, process, and store personal data. However, due to political disagreements and industry concerns, the bill was not passed. Subsequently, the government proposed a revised version known as the Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Bill, which remains under parliamentary discussion. These legislative efforts demonstrate India’s growing commitment to building a robust data protection framework, though the process remains ongoing.

The evolution of privacy laws also reflects a changing public mindset. Awareness about data protection has increased, particularly among urban and educated citizens, who are more conscious of online security and surveillance risks. However, a significant portion of the population continues to prioritize access to government benefits and convenience over privacy concerns, a behavior known as the privacy paradox. This paradox illustrates the challenge of fostering privacy awareness in a country where socio-economic needs often outweigh abstract rights.

Conditions and Limits of the Right to Privacy

While privacy is recognized as a fundamental right, the Supreme Court clarified that it is not absolute. For any government interference or surveillance of private data to be lawful, three essential conditions must be met:

  • Legality: The action must be backed by a valid and existing law.
  • Legitimate State Aim: The interference must serve a legitimate purpose, such as national security or public order.
  • Proportionality: The extent of data collection or intrusion must be reasonable and proportionate to the objective.

    • Various government agencies are authorized under the Indian Telegraph Act and related laws to intercept communications, but certain agencies require additional permissions before doing so. These overlapping authorities have led to grey areas in interpretation, especially regarding when and how state surveillance may be justified. This ambiguity continues to raise questions about accountability, oversight, and misuse of surveillance powers.

    Conclusion

    India’s data privacy landscape is still in transition, shaped by constitutional principles, judicial interpretation, and legislative initiatives. The recognition of privacy as a fundamental right marked a historic step toward protecting individual freedoms in the digital age. However, the country continues to face challenges in balancing personal liberty with legitimate state interests such as security, governance, and development. The introduction of new data protection laws like the DPDP Bill signifies India’s progress toward establishing a clear and accountable data governance framework. Moving forward, ensuring public awareness, transparency, and proportional regulation will be essential to protect privacy in an increasingly digitized society.
