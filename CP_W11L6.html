Privacy: The Indian Way - Part 3

India’s journey toward ensuring data privacy and protection has evolved alongside its digital transformation, driven by the need to balance individual rights, technological progress, and national interests. The growing dependence on digital platforms, government databases, and online transactions has made the protection of personal information a crucial policy concern. To safeguard citizens’ privacy and regulate data usage, the Indian government initiated legal and institutional reforms through proposed legislation such as the Personal Data Protection (PDP) Bill and its later version, the Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Bill.

The **Personal Data Protection Bill of 2017** was introduced after the Justice B.N. Srikrishna Committee’s recommendations, following the Supreme Court’s 2017 judgment recognizing privacy as a fundamental right under Article 21 of the Constitution. The PDP Bill aimed to create a comprehensive framework to govern the collection, storage, and processing of personal data. It defined three major stakeholders—data principals, who are individuals whose data is collected; data fiduciaries, who determine how and why the data is processed; and data processors, who handle data on behalf of fiduciaries. The Bill also categorized data into three types—personal, sensitive, and critical personal data—and introduced the concept of data localization, requiring certain types of data to be stored within India to ensure sovereignty and protection from foreign access.

However, as the bill progressed, concerns arose about excessive government powers. Amendments proposed by the government allowed it to access personal data under broad exceptions such as national security and public order. These provisions weakened public trust and led to widespread criticism from civil society, privacy advocates, and opposition leaders. As a result, the PDP Bill was eventually withdrawn and replaced by the Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Bill, which simplified the structure and focused only on personally identifiable information, removing the earlier detailed classifications of data.

The **DPDP Bill** is designed to make compliance easier for businesses while maintaining essential privacy safeguards. It reduces the requirement for strict data localization, allowing cross-border data transfers to trusted nations. This move was made to support international trade and reduce operational burdens on multinational corporations. However, it has also sparked debate over whether such relaxation compromises citizens’ data security. Supporters of localization argue that storing data within the country ensures better enforcement under Indian laws, fosters domestic data analytics industries, and strengthens national sovereignty. Critics, on the other hand, claim that it increases costs for companies, limits global efficiency, and could hinder innovation by making India less attractive for global tech firms.

Despite these debates, the DPDP framework maintains the principle of transparency, accountability, and consent-based data processing. It emphasizes that individuals must be informed about how their data is being used and have the right to withdraw consent. The law also provides for the establishment of a Data Protection Board of India, responsible for monitoring compliance and handling grievances. Yet, challenges remain—especially regarding the broad exemptions granted to the state for purposes such as law enforcement, security, and public interest, which could potentially undermine individual privacy.

The overall discussion on India’s data protection journey reveals a deep tension between privacy, national security, and economic progress. On one hand, the government seeks to use data to drive innovation, improve service delivery, and support a thriving digital economy. On the other, it must ensure that citizens’ personal information is not misused or exploited. The ongoing policy evolution shows India’s attempt to create a balanced digital governance model that supports growth without compromising individual rights.

In conclusion, India’s data protection framework continues to evolve as the country strives to protect personal liberty in an era where data is the new oil. The challenge lies in achieving a balanced approach that protects privacy, supports innovation, and ensures accountability. The Digital Personal Data Protection Bill marks an important milestone in this effort, representing India’s commitment to building a secure, transparent, and citizen-centric digital future.
