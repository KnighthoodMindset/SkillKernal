Cybersecurity industry perspective - Part 2

The concept of a Virtual Private Network (VPN is central to modern cybersecurity for organizations with multiple branches. Instead of replicating servers and data at each location, companies create a central repository for data. Branches connect to this repository via a private network. Physically laying dedicated lines between branches would be expensive, so companies use the internet to establish a secure encrypted tunnel — this is the essence of a VPN. A VPN server (or Virtual Private Server, VPS) assigns private IP addresses, making remote users appear as though they are part of the office network, allowing them to securely access resources regardless of their location. From a hacker’s perspective, a VPN is considered the "key to the kingdom" — compromising it can allow undetected access to an organization’s network. Large organizations often use interconnected VPN servers, and the network's security depends on its weakest link.

Cyber attackers follow a structured process similar to military operations, often referred to as the cyber kill chain. This begins with reconnaissance to gather information about the target, identifying vulnerabilities, and selecting the optimal method and tools for intrusion. Once access is gained, attackers deliver their payload, maintain persistence, and continue operations without detection. Attackers may operate randomly, sending mass phishing attempts hoping to exploit weak systems, or conduct targeted cyber espionage focused on a single victim, which requires more resources and specialized tools. Phishing is general and random, while spear phishing is targeted and tailored to a specific individual, requiring deeper reconnaissance.

Key concepts in cyberattacks include vulnerability, exploit, and payload. A vulnerability is a weakness in a system, such as flaws in software like Microsoft Word macros. An exploit is the method used to take advantage of that weakness, and a payload is the malicious software delivered to execute the attacker’s objectives. Payloads, often Remote Administration Tools (RATs), give attackers control over compromised systems and can be bought cheaply on the dark web. RATs are disguised as legitimate software, and attackers often use social engineering to trick users into enabling them, for example, by sending malicious attachments disguised as updates or business documents.

A common form of attack involves Trojan malware, such as Qbot, which spreads via deceptive emails containing zipped files that hide malicious macros. Once executed, these install additional malicious components in multiple stages, giving attackers control over the system. These attacks may be part of building a botnet, which can then be monetized through ransomware, data theft, or click fraud. Social engineering plays a major role in convincing victims to activate malicious files, often by creating urgency or using familiar communication styles.

Advanced threats, called Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs), are highly targeted and resource-intensive operations, often motivated by espionage, disruption, or destruction. APTs focus on a single target for a prolonged period, adapting tactics continuously to avoid detection. Examples include state-sponsored attacks, such as efforts to steal vaccine data during the COVID-19 pandemic. Cyberattacks can be categorized into the "three Ds": disrupt (interfere with normal functioning), degrade (reduce efficiency), and destroy (cause total damage).

Common cyberattack types include malware distribution, web-based attacks, phishing, denial-of-service (DoS), identity theft, data breaches, insider threats, botnets, ransomware, espionage, and cryptojacking — the latter being unauthorized cryptocurrency mining using a victim’s resources. Attackers often work in cybercrime markets on the dark web, selling tools like RATs, stealers (such as Vidar, which steals credentials), and malware kits. These markets operate like businesses, with constant updates to evade detection. The security landscape is dynamic, with new vulnerabilities discovered daily, making cybersecurity an ongoing challenge.

Finally, cyberattack operations rely heavily on deception, persistence, and adaptability, and defending against them requires not only technical solutions but also awareness of tactics such as social engineering, phishing, and the use of disguised malware. Understanding these attack methodologies is essential to building robust cybersecurity defenses.
