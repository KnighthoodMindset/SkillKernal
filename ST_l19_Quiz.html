Software testing - lecture 19 - Quiz

What do finite state machines (FSMs) represent in software testing?





FSMs represent a system as a finite graph where nodes are states and edges are transitions.

In FSMs, what do edges (transitions) represent?





Edges in FSMs represent transitions caused by execution of program statements or actions.

What role do guards or preconditions play in FSM transitions?





Guards or preconditions control the conditions under which transitions between states can occur.

Which structural coverage criteria are commonly applied in FSM testing?





Node, edge, edge pair, and specified path coverage are commonly applied, while prime path is less useful.

Why is data flow analysis in FSMs considered complex?





Data flow analysis is complex since variable definitions and uses can appear in node actions or guarded transitions.

How can FSMs be applied to abstract data types like queues?





FSMs model queue states (empty, partially filled, full) and transitions caused by enqueue and dequeue operations.

Which operations in a queue FSM do not change the state?





Operations like checking if the queue is empty or full do not alter the FSM state.

How do UML diagrams support FSM-based testing?





UML diagrams such as state machines, state charts, and activity diagrams provide graph-like models for applying coverage criteria.

What advantage do FSMs have over control flow or call graphs?





FSMs explicitly include variable values in states, offering a more detailed representation than control flow or call graphs.

What is the role of FSMs in preparing for logical predicate-based testing?





FSMs model complex state-dependent behaviors, preparing the foundation for logical predicate-based testing.
